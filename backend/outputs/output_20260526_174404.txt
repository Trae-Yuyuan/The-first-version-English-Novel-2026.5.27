An old tungsten filament lamp hung from a black wire in the center of the room, flickering with a dim glow.

The tranquil atmosphere, like ink dripping into clear water, spread and suffused through the room.

At the very center of the room stood a large, worn round table, its surface mottled and decayed. On the table sat a small, intricately patterned clock, ticking away.

Around the table sat ten individuals, each dressed in varied attire, their clothes somewhat shabby and their faces smeared with dust.

Some lay slumped on the tabletop, others leaned back in their chairs, all in a deep sleep.

Beside these ten stood a man in a black suit, wearing a goat-head mask.

His gaze pierced through the worn goat-head mask, fixed intently on the ten sleepers.

The clock on the table chimed, its minute and hour hands both pointing to “twelve.”

From far beyond the room, a deep bell tolled.

At that same moment, the ten men and women seated around the round table slowly awakened.

As they regained consciousness, they first looked around in confusion, then glanced at each other with bewilderment.

It seemed no one remembered how they had come to be there.

“Good morning, nine of you,” the goat-head spoke first. “I am pleased to meet you here. You have slept before me for twelve hours.”

The man’s attire was truly eerie, startling everyone under the dim light.

His mask seemed to be made from a real goat’s head, its hair yellowed and blackened, matted and tangled together.

Two hollow holes had been cut for the eyes, revealing his cunning gaze.

Every gesture he made exuded not only the unique musky smell of a goat but also a faint, rotting odor.

A man with tattooed arms froze for a few seconds before finally realizing the absurdity of the situation. Hesitantly, he asked the goat-head, “Who... are you?”

“I believe you all share this question, so let me introduce myself to the nine of you,” the goat-head said, waving his hands excitedly, as if he had long prepared an answer.

A young man named Qi Xia sat farthest from the goat-head, quickly surveying the room. After a moment, his expression grew grave.

Strange. This room was truly strange.

There was no door—only walls on all four sides.

In other words, the room’s walls, ceiling, and floor were all sealed, with a table placed in the center.

So how had they gotten here?

Could it be that they had been brought in first, and then the walls were built afterward?

Qi Xia looked around again. The floor, walls, and ceiling all bore crisscrossing lines, dividing them into large squares.

Another thing that troubled Qi Xia was the goat-head’s phrase “nine of you.”

No matter how he counted, there were ten people seated around the round table. Including the goat-head himself, that made eleven in the room.

What did “nine” mean?

He reached into his pocket and, as expected, found his phone had been taken.

“There’s no need for introductions,” a cold woman said to the goat-head. “I advise you to stop your actions immediately. I suspect you have detained us for over twenty-four hours, constituting illegal detention. Every word you say now will be recorded and used as evidence against you.”

As she spoke, she disdainfully rubbed at the dust on her arm, as if she hated being dirtied more than being imprisoned.

Her words sobered the others. Whoever this was, kidnapping ten people alone had already crossed the line of the law.

“Wait...” a middle-aged man in a white coat interrupted the train of thought. He looked slowly at the cold woman and asked, “We’ve all just woken up. How do you know we’ve been detained for twenty-four hours?” His tone was steady and forceful, but he hit the nail on the head.

The cold woman calmly pointed at the clock on the table. “The clock here points to twelve, but I have a habit of sleeping late. The last time I checked the clock at home, it was already twelve. That means we’ve been detained for at least twelve hours,” she replied.
